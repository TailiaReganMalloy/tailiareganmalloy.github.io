## Dirty Dozen Behaviors
Lack of communication: Information is not passed along correctly. 
Complacency: Becoming overconfident and losing awareness of potential dangers. 
Lack of knowledge: Not understanding systems or procedures correctly. 
Distraction: Anything that takes a person's attention away from their task. 
Lack of teamwork: Individuals not sharing information or supporting each other. 
Fatigue: Impaired judgment and slowed reaction times due to tiredness. 
Lack of resources: Not having enough personnel, tools, or time to do the job safely. 
Pressure: Being pushed to work faster, sometimes leading to shortcuts. 
Lack of assertiveness: Not voicing concerns or speaking up. 
Stress: Arising from deadlines, challenging conditions, or personal issues. 
Lack of awareness: Being oblivious to what is happening around you. 
Norms: Unwritten rules and practices that can sometimes lead to unsafe behaviors. 

Academic research within the Dutch National Cybersecurity Center involves doing their own research and collaborating with researchers within academia. 'The human in cybersecurity: The issue or the solution' argues for a compassionate education around security rather than a punishing name/shame approach. # Maggie Zhang presentation 

Quantifiable Security: Challenges and Opportunities in the Age of AI

Measurements are difficult to form meaningfully for cybersecurity contexts. 

Common vulnerability scorind system combines multiple metrics of vulnerability to try and get an overall view of how vulnerable a system is. 

When designing measurements, it is important to remember that the information that is generated will eventually be observed by a human, at least in some form. 

Example of a diverse system being more resillient to attacks potentially because of different risks. However the metric of resilliency can be difficult to apply in real life because an attacker could have access to multiple attack strategies, making the differences irrelevant. 
- Applied concepts from ecology, the richness of species in diversity onto measuing the diversity of real systems. 

'Detecting common attack surfaces': Try to refine the concept of diversity through measurements of the similarity of methods tht can be used at attacks, and what the ultimate attack system is. 

Vulernabilities exist in the real world but not all of them are discovered or actually attacked. This raises the question of what features of codebases make them more or less likely to have a vulnerability discovered in them. 

Issues for the databases that were used to analyze whether a vulnerability was found in a github repository so some data cleaning methods were necessary to imperove it. 

GAI mentioned: It is possible to have fine-tuned LLMs predict the vulnerability of code bases, and they can be good in some ways but other issues. One of the issues is that there are discrepancies in the dataset of code bases, so the biases end up being reflected in the fine-tuned LLMs. 

To address this issue Maggie developed a tool to highlight the importance of vulnerability entities so that people using the tool can understand what areas of the vulnerability may be important. 

NVD dataset has had issues of lack of many different people rating vulnerabilities, but this can be problematic because different people have different interests in why they would want a vulnerability to be higher or lower than what someone else would rate it. 

LLMs are also not very great at doing vulnerability quantifications, often making logical mistakes that don't make sense given the way that vulnerabilities function. Instead of just LLMs the work used logical rules to ensure consistency in quantifications which improved performance. This can be used to make predictions of the impact likelihood of happening and the damage that would be done, as seperate measures.
