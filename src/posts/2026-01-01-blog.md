Over the past few years, my research has focused on a simple but increasingly urgent question: how do humans interact with large language models (LLMs) in cybersecurity contexts, and what does that mean for both attackers and defenders?

Cybersecurity has always been a fundamentally human problem. While we often frame it in terms of systems, networks, and vulnerabilities, the reality is that many of the most successful attacks—especially phishing and spear-phishing—exploit human cognition rather than technical flaws. What is changing now is that LLMs are entering this space not just as tools, but as active participants in these interactions.

Recent work across the field shows that generative AI is already transforming social engineering by enabling highly realistic, personalized, and scalable attacks (Springer￼). Phishing, in particular, has become more adaptive and convincing because LLMs can mimic tone, context, and intent at a level that was previously difficult to achieve. At the same time, these same systems are being used to strengthen defenses, creating a complex and evolving human–AI ecosystem.

My work sits directly at this intersection.

In my research, I approach cybersecurity as a dynamic interaction space involving three key roles: attackers, defenders, and targets. Traditionally, these interactions were already complex, shaped by human decision-making, biases, and learning processes. With the introduction of LLMs—and especially LLM-based agents—this complexity expands significantly. We are no longer studying just human behavior, but human–AI collaboration and, in some cases, human–AI adversarial dynamics.

To make sense of this, I draw heavily on cognitive science. The central idea is that if we can model how people think, learn, and make decisions in security-critical situations, we can design better AI systems—both for training and for defense. This aligns with a broader shift in the field toward human-centered cybersecurity, where understanding user behavior is as important as building technical safeguards.

One of my earlier research efforts explored how LLMs can be used for anti-phishing training. In an online experiment, we asked hundreds of participants to classify emails as phishing or legitimate while interacting with an LLM-based tutor. What we found was that training becomes significantly more effective when it is personalized and grounded in cognitive models. By integrating techniques such as instance-based learning, we were able to adapt feedback and examples to each user’s behavior, improving their ability to detect phishing attempts over time. This reflects a growing consensus that static training approaches are insufficient in the face of evolving AI-generated threats (ScienceDirect￼).

However, the story does not end with defense.

In my current work, I examine how LLMs are used to generate spear-phishing websites—arguably one of the most sophisticated forms of social engineering. These attacks go beyond deceptive emails to create fully functional, highly convincing replicas of legitimate platforms. What is particularly interesting is the comparison between different modes of generation: fully automated LLM agents versus human–LLM collaborative workflows.

What I observe is that collaboration matters. When humans and LLMs work together—especially in programming or iterative prompting contexts—the resulting phishing artifacts can become more targeted, more coherent, and ultimately more dangerous. This reflects a broader trend in cybersecurity where LLMs act as “capability multipliers,” lowering the barrier to entry while amplifying attacker effectiveness.

At the same time, similar collaboration dynamics are being explored on the defensive side. Research shows that human–AI teaming can improve decision-making accuracy and even support long-term skill development in cybersecurity tasks, though it also introduces risks such as over-reliance on AI outputs (arXiv￼). This duality—augmentation versus dependence—is something I consider central to the future of human–LLM interaction.

Another important dimension of my work involves leveraging existing datasets. Human behavior data in cybersecurity is expensive and time-consuming to collect, yet there is a wealth of underutilized datasets from prior studies. By reusing and reanalyzing these datasets, we can accelerate research, test new hypotheses, and build more robust models without always starting from scratch. This is particularly valuable when studying emerging phenomena like LLM-generated attacks.

Looking ahead, my future research focuses on detection—specifically, identifying spear-phishing websites generated either by LLMs alone or through human–AI collaboration. This is a challenging problem because these artifacts are designed to closely mimic legitimate systems, often blending human intuition with machine-generated precision. Emerging approaches in the field, including multi-agent LLM systems for phishing detection, suggest that AI may also play a key role in identifying intent and deception at scale.

Ultimately, my work is driven by a broader perspective: cybersecurity is evolving into a human–AI interaction problem. LLMs are not just tools we use; they are shaping how attacks are created, how defenses are designed, and how people make decisions in high-stakes environments.

If we want to build resilient systems, we need to understand not only what these models can do, but how humans work with them—how they trust them, question them, learn from them, and sometimes misuse them.

That intersection is where the future of cybersecurity will be defined.
